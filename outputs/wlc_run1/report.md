Dulles International Airport Main Terminal is the original passenger terminal building at Washington Dulles International Airport in Virginia, United States.[1][2] Developed in the late 1950s and opened in 1962, it was commissioned for the Federal Aviation Agency (FAA) and conceived as a jet‑age gateway focused on passenger processing rather than a conventional gateside concourse.[1][3][4] Architect Eero Saarinen and Associates designed the terminal around a system of mobile lounges that would carry travelers between the terminal and their aircraft, minimizing walking distances.[3][4][5] The building’s most prominent architectural feature is a long, curving, cable‑supported roof carried by canted reinforced‑concrete piers, creating a large, largely uninterrupted interior hall framed by extensive glass curtain walls.[2][6][7][8] Since opening, the terminal has been expanded and modernized, including a 1996 extension to its full design length, a main‑terminal improvement project completed in 2005, and the construction of an underground AeroTrain station and automated people‑mover system that opened in 2010.[9][10][11][12] Preservation and planning documents identify the main terminal as historically and architecturally significant, and it has been determined eligible for listing on the U.S. National Register of Historic Places.[2][13][14]

## History and planning

Planning for a new Washington‑area airport accelerated in the mid‑1950s as federal officials sought a jet‑age facility and a terminal concept centered on passenger processing rather than long gate concourses.[4][5] An institutional history of the metropolitan Washington airports records that a project team was assembled in 1958 to plan, design, build, and open what became Washington Dulles International Airport.[15] The airport was developed under the Federal Aviation Agency (FAA), which is identified in later preservation reporting as the federal entity that developed Dulles, including its main terminal, in 1962.[1][16]

Docomomo US states that Eero Saarinen and Associates were chosen as architects for the main terminal in 1958, with Ammann & Whitney selected as engineers.[2][3] This commission is also reflected in FAA historic‑resource documentation, which credits Eero Saarinen as the architect and Ammann & Whitney as consulting engineers for the main terminal building.[7][8]

Washington Dulles International Airport opened in 1962.[1][2] Docomomo US reports that the airport officially opened and was formally dedicated by U.S. President John F. Kennedy on 17 November 1962.[2] The terminal’s design and mobile‑lounge concept were presented as a new model for jet‑age passenger handling at that time.[4][5]

In 1987, control of Washington Dulles International Airport was transferred from the federal government to the Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority (MWAA).[17][18] MWAA’s institutional history notes that this transfer placed Dulles, including the main terminal, under the authority’s capital‑program framework for expansion and modernization.[17][18]

## Design and architecture

The main terminal was designed by Eero Saarinen and Associates for the Federal Aviation Agency.[2][3] Saarinen died in 1961, before the airport opened.[19] An AIA Journal reference identifies “Dulles International Airport, Chantilly, Va.” as being completed in 1962 by Kevin Roche John Dinkeloo & Associates, Saarinen’s successor firm, indicating that the latter brought the project to completion after Saarinen’s death.[16]

### Structural concept and materials

A defining architectural and structural feature of the terminal is its roof system. Docomomo US describes the roof as concrete panels supported by catenary cables spanning between canted supports, used to create a large uninterrupted interior space for passenger circulation.[2][6] An FAA environmental‑assessment appendix similarly characterizes the roof as a cable‑supported system and identifies the terminal’s principal materials as concrete (notably the inclined structural supports), glass curtain walls, and steel elements associated with the cable roof.[7][8] 

A structural case study on the building explains that the roof is carried by a system of steel cables spanning between two rows of inclined reinforced‑concrete supports; the cables form catenary‑like curves anchored into the ground, and the tilted concrete supports resist the resulting horizontal forces while transferring loads to the foundations.[8][20] The study notes that the cable system supports a relatively thin concrete roof slab or shell, enabling a long‑span roof and an expansive interior volume.[8][20] In some technical descriptions the plan arrangement of the cable system is described as radial, whereas other sources emphasize the catenary or catenary‑like form of the cables; available evidence does not fully reconcile these terminologies, and this article follows each cited source’s wording.[2][7][8]

Multiple architectural accounts emphasize the long, transparent glass walls that line the sides of the terminal, set back beneath the deep roof overhang to admit daylight and maintain visual continuity between interior and exterior spaces.[7][21][22] The inclined concrete supports and the hovering roof edge contribute to an impression that the roof floats above the glazed enclosure.[21][22]

### Form, style, and design intent

Historical and critical sources describe the main terminal as a jet‑age and mid‑20th‑century modern landmark. Docomomo US, FAA documentation, and architectural histories present the building as a significant example of modernist airport design, highlighting its elongated east–west volume, sculptural cable‑supported roof, canted concrete piers, and glass curtain walls.[2][7][13][23] 

A PBS American Masters feature interprets the sweeping roof surface and inclined concrete supports as a formal language expressing speed, motion, and technological optimism in the context of the jet age.[21][22] The same feature characterizes the terminal as a break from earlier airport types: instead of long finger concourses at the aircraft edge, Dulles used a single monumental hall paired with mobile lounges to handle passengers.[5][21] 

An architecture‑history entry and a Modernist Collection essay both situate the terminal within Saarinen’s late career, describing it as an important work that combines structural expression, sculptural form, and integrated interior design.[16][23][24] These accounts highlight Saarinen’s “total design” approach, in which the building’s form, structural system, furnishings, ticket counters, signage, and even vehicles were coordinated to project an image of modern technology and efficiency.[21][23][24]

## Construction and opening

Docomomo US reports that construction of Washington Dulles International Airport began in 1958 and that the airport officially opened and was dedicated by President John F. Kennedy on 17 November 1962.[2] A popular historical article from Ghosts of DC states that construction ran from 1959 to 1962 and cost US$110 million.[25] A Historypin entry, using an archival construction photograph credited to the Library of Virginia, notes that the main terminal was designed in 1958 by Eero Saarinen, with metadata including a 1962 date for the completed facility.[24]

These sources differ slightly on the exact year in which construction began (1958 versus 1959), and the available evidence set does not include a single primary contract record that resolves the discrepancy.[2][24][25] All agree, however, that the main terminal was constructed in the late 1950s and completed in time for the airport’s 1962 opening and formal dedication.[1][2][24][25]

## Renovations and expansions

### 1990s lengthening and area growth

MWAA’s overview describes the original main terminal as a compact two‑level structure 600 feet long and 200 feet wide, designed by Eero Saarinen.[9][26] The same overview states that in 1996 the Airports Authority completed an “architectural milestone” by expanding the original 1962 terminal to its design length.[9] The expanded terminal is described as 1,240 feet in length with a floor area of about 1.1 million square feet.[9][26]

Subsequent baseline descriptions from MWAA and an FAA‑related Request for Information (RFI) page characterize the Main Terminal as having approximately 1.3 million square feet of floor space, reflecting later additions and reconfigurations.[11][14][27] The RFI description also notes that the International Arrivals Building (a related facility) has nearly 268,000 square feet of space.[27]

### Main Terminal Improvement Project (completed 2005)

As part of MWAA’s Dulles Development (D2) capital program, a “Main Terminal Improvement Project” was undertaken. An MWAA project page lists this project with a completion date of 2005 and a program cost of US$174.2 million.[10] MWAA’s history‑and‑facts overview identifies the D2 program as encompassing several major facilities, including a new air traffic control tower, expanded airline gates, a fourth runway, and the AeroTrain system, situating the main‑terminal improvements within a broader airport modernization effort.[28][29]

### Main Terminal AeroTrain Station (completed 2009, opened 2010)

A major expansion on the airfield side of the Main Terminal was the Main Terminal AeroTrain Station, constructed as part of the D2 program. MWAA’s project description characterizes the station as a “major expansion on the airfield side of the Main Terminal,” built approximately 60 feet below ground in order not to obstruct views of Saarinen’s historic terminal.[11][12][30] The station spans the entire length of the Main Terminal—about 1,600 feet—and is organized on four levels with a side‑center‑side platform configuration.[11][30]

The same MWAA project page states that the station and the AeroTrain System were completed in 2009 and opened to passengers on 26 January 2010.[11][12][31] SOM, which worked on the AeroTrain system, likewise records a design finish year of 2009 and a completion year of 2010 for the main‑terminal station and notes that the AeroTrain has provided rail and pedestrian links between the main terminal and the midfield concourses since opening in 2010.[31][32]

A central operational component of the project was the reconfiguration of passenger screening. MWAA explains that moving passenger screening operations to a new Security Mezzanine associated with the AeroTrain station would alleviate congestion from the previous screening location on the main terminal ticketing level, and that the Security Mezzanine could be reached directly from baggage‑claim areas and parking facilities.[12][30][33]

### Other improvements and planning for redevelopment

MWAA has also pursued more focused improvements within the main terminal. A procurement posting for IFB‑18‑31790, titled “Main Terminal Baggage Basement Improvements, Dulles Airport,” required the contractor to furnish all necessary labor, materials, tools, equipment, and supervision to construct specified improvements to the Main Terminal baggage basement; the notice was last updated on 9 January 2019.[34]

Recent planning documents describe prospective redevelopment of the terminal and concourse complex. An MWAA public notice for a Draft Programmatic Environmental Assessment (PEA) states that the proposed action includes redevelopment of the terminal/concourse facilities at Washington Dulles International Airport and associated development.[35][36] A subsequent MWAA notice records that the Federal Aviation Administration issued a Finding of No Significant Impact/Record of Decision (FONSI/ROD) for IAD terminal concourse redevelopment on 4 May 2026.[37]

Contemporary reporting and commentary outline ambitious future plans affecting the main terminal. A Washington Business Journal article and a summary circulated by AviationCircle describe an MWAA plan on the order of US$22 billion through 2034 that would eliminate mobile lounges and expand the main terminal.[38][39][40] The AviationCircle summary lists components such as a US$6.2 billion expansion of the main terminal combined with renovation of Concourse A starting in April 2027, and a US$3.75 billion AeroTrain extension and underground connector linking the terminal to a new Concourse D.[39][40][41] An Airport Architecture Substack article likewise describes a proposed program involving terminal expansion, four new linear concourses, new subterranean circulation without Direct Jet Transports (mobile lounges), and a target completion by 2034.[42][43]

These future‑oriented figures and component budgets are reported in news and commentary sources and are not fully cross‑verified by primary MWAA planning documents in the available evidence.[38][39][40][42][43] They nonetheless indicate a planning trajectory in which the historic main terminal would be expanded and tied into new concourses and underground circulation systems, with mobile lounges ultimately phased out.[14][39][42]

## Layout and functions

### Overall airport configuration

MWAA’s overview of Dulles describes the airport as having a Main Terminal and four midfield concourses (A, B, C, and D).[14][44] Passengers can reach the midfield concourses from the Main Terminal either via the underground AeroTrain automated people mover system or via mobile lounges that transport passengers to and from the Main Terminal.[14][44][45] The same overview and related MWAA materials state that there are six aircraft gates referred to as the Z Gates, associated with the main‑terminal building.[14][45][46]

The RFI baseline description identifies the Main Terminal as having approximately 1.3 million square feet of floor space and describes the International Arrivals Building as a nearly 268,000‑square‑foot facility providing customs, agriculture, and immigration services, with capacity to process up to 2,400 passengers per hour.[11][27][47]

### Internal levels and passenger facilities

Official and third‑party guides describe the main terminal as a two‑level building with departures (ticketing/check‑in) functions on the upper level and arrivals (baggage‑claim) functions on the lower level.[9][26][48][49] A NerdWallet airport guide summarizes this configuration as departures upstairs and arrivals downstairs, and a separate layout guide notes that the upper level contains four ticketing areas plus two primary security checkpoint areas, East and West.[48][49][50]

Flydulles’ security‑information page states that there are three TSA security checkpoints at Dulles—East, West, and TSA PreCheck—and that all are accessed from the Departures (Ticketing/Check‑In) level.[51][52] Information‑desk listings further indicate that information desks are located on the Departures (Ticketing) level, on the Arrivals (Baggage‑Claim) level, and just beyond the East and West security checkpoints, reinforcing the functional separation between the levels.[53][54]

The International Arrivals Building, while a distinct structure within the complex, is closely linked to the main‑terminal system. MWAA’s RFI description notes that it provides customs, agriculture, and immigration facilities and can serve up to 2,400 passengers per hour.[27][47] Flydulles states that all arriving international passengers and luggage must be screened by U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP), and that passengers connecting to another flight must re‑check their luggage and undergo Transportation Security Administration (TSA) screening.[55][56] CBP’s Port of Washington–Dulles contact page locates the port on the Main Terminal Lower Level, indicating that federal inspection services are physically integrated with the main‑terminal complex.[52][57]

### Ground transportation and Metrorail connection

The main terminal also functions as the hub for landside transportation connections. MWAA describes the Dulles Corridor Metrorail project as a 23.1‑mile line connecting the Dulles corridor and Washington Dulles International Airport.[58] The Dulles Airport Metrorail Station, which serves the Washington Metro Silver Line, is connected to the main terminal by an indoor pedestrian tunnel with moving sidewalks.[59][60][61] Flydulles notes that this tunnel provides convenient access to the terminal’s ticketing and baggage‑claim levels and links the terminal to Parking Garage 1, with signage guiding passengers to the tunnel.[59][60][61][62]

### AeroTrain and mobile‑lounge interface

MWAA and Flydulles materials describe AeroTrain as the automated airport train system for Washington Dulles International Airport, connecting passengers between the Terminal and the midfield concourses.[59][63] The system consists of fully automated three‑car trains running in underground tunnels linking the Main Terminal and concourses A, B, and C.[59][63][64] The station in the main terminal is located just beyond the TSA security‑screening checkpoints.[63][65]

There is no AeroTrain station at Concourse D. MWAA’s overview notes that passengers can reach Concourse D by walking through Concourse C or by riding a mobile lounge shuttle.[63][66] The same overview states that the airport operates 49 mobile lounges.[44][67] MWAA’s descriptions emphasize that passengers may use either the underground AeroTrain automated people mover or mobile lounges to travel between the main terminal and the midfield concourses, reflecting a hybrid circulation system.[14][44][63][66]

The available evidence provides high‑level descriptions of the main terminal’s layout and functions but does not include comprehensive, authoritative diagrams for all interior spaces or detailed curbside and ground‑transportation arrangements. Accordingly, this article focuses on the major elements that are explicitly documented in the cited sources: the two‑level passenger configuration, security and information‑desk locations, customs and immigration facilities, and the connections to the AeroTrain system, mobile lounges, Metrorail station, and parking.[9][14][26][48][59]

## Operations and evolution of passenger circulation

### Original mobile‑lounge concept

The original terminal concept at Dulles emphasized minimizing walking distances and using mobile lounges as the primary means of moving passengers between the landside terminal and aircraft. Historical analyses and the official airport history describe the main terminal as a passenger‑processing gateway feeding mobile‑lounge boarding points, rather than a building with long concourses at the gate line.[4][5][21][68] 

MWAA’s history page states that when the airport opened in 1962, passengers had to walk only about 200 feet from the time they entered the terminal until they were seated in a mobile lounge to travel to their aircraft.[26][69] The same history notes that in 1962 the feature that made the new airport unique from the public’s viewpoint was the set of specially designed mobile lounges used to transport passengers.[66][70] 

Brian Edwards’s study of modern airport terminals and other architectural commentaries similarly characterize the main terminal as organized around the mobile‑lounge system, with the single monumental hall functioning as a processing and gathering space rather than a concourse at the gate edge.[5][23][68]

### Shift to AeroTrain and hybrid circulation

Over subsequent decades, Dulles added midfield concourses and eventually an underground people‑mover system. MWAA’s overview and the RFI baseline describe the airport as having a main terminal and four midfield concourses (A, B, C, and D), which can be reached via the underground AeroTrain or via mobile lounges.[14][44][47] 

Flydulles’ AeroTrain information page describes AeroTrain as the automated airport train system for Washington Dulles International Airport, connecting passengers between the Terminal and the midfield concourses using fully automated three‑car trains.[59][63][64] According to Flydulles, AeroTrain has been in operation since 2010, carrying passengers between the Terminal and the midfield gates for domestic and international departing flights and domestic arriving flights; most passengers arriving on international flights do not ride AeroTrain because they must first clear U.S. customs.[59][63][71][72]

MWAA’s project description for the Main Terminal AeroTrain Station states that the station and the AeroTrain System were completed in 2009 and opened on 26 January 2010.[11][31] MWAA’s D2 project narrative and an MWAA AeroTrain‑system description note that the AeroTrain replaced most of the mobile‑lounge system for movements between the Main Terminal and the midfield concourses, while still coexisting with mobile lounges.[31][73][74] A Transport Politic article on the day AeroTrain opened argues that the system effectively took over the main role of transporting passengers between the terminal and the midfield concourses, with mobile lounges relegated to more limited functions.[74]

At the same time, MWAA’s overview continues to list mobile lounges as an option for reaching the midfield concourses and notes that there is no AeroTrain station at Concourse D, which is instead accessible by walking through Concourse C or by mobile lounge shuttle.[44][63][66] MWAA also notes that the airport has a fleet of 49 mobile lounges.[44][67] Taken together, these sources indicate that AeroTrain has become the primary circulation system for many passengers, particularly to concourses A and B, while mobile lounges remain in service for certain routes and operational scenarios, especially where AeroTrain does not provide direct access.[14][44][59][63][66][74]

### Security screening and federal inspection services

The opening of the Main Terminal AeroTrain Station was accompanied by a reconfiguration of passenger security screening. MWAA’s project description states that the station is located just beyond the TSA security‑screening checkpoints and that passenger screening operations would be moved to a new Security Mezzanine to alleviate congestion from the previous screening location on the main terminal ticketing level.[12][30][33][65] The Security Mezzanine is described as being accessible from baggage‑claim areas and parking facilities, consolidating screening functions in a zone directly connected to the AeroTrain station.[12][30][33]

Flydulles’ security‑information page states that there are three TSA security checkpoints—East, West, and TSA PreCheck—and that all are accessed from the Departures (Ticketing/Check‑In) level.[51][52] Flydulles also notes that passengers connecting from an arriving international flight to another flight must re‑check their luggage and go through TSA screening.[55][56]

Customs and immigration operations for arriving international passengers are handled primarily in the International Arrivals Building and associated facilities. The RFI description notes that the International Arrivals Building provides customs, agriculture, and immigration facilities and can process up to 2,400 passengers per hour.[27][47] Flydulles states that all arriving international passengers and luggage must be screened by U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP), and CBP’s Port of Washington–Dulles page locates the port on the Main Terminal Lower Level.[52][55][57] Flydulles also emphasizes that passengers connecting onward after international arrival must re‑check luggage and undergo TSA screening, underscoring the integrated sequence of federal inspection and security processes linked to the main terminal.[55][56]

The available evidence does not provide a detailed year‑by‑year chronology of all operational changes in the main terminal, such as specific post‑2001 security upgrades or airline‑assignment shifts. The account here therefore focuses on elements that are explicitly documented: the original mobile‑lounge‑based circulation model, the introduction and expansion of AeroTrain, the coexistence of AeroTrain and mobile lounges, and the relocation and integration of security and federal inspection facilities within the main‑terminal complex.[4][14][27][44][59]

## Cultural significance and critical reception

Docomomo US identifies the Washington Dulles International Airport Main Terminal as a significant modern building commissioned by the Federal Aviation Agency and designed by Eero Saarinen and Associates, noting its concrete‑panel roof supported by catenary cables as a key architectural feature.[2][3][6] The organization’s register entry records that the terminal received an American Institute of Architects (AIA) Honor Award in 1966 and that it was determined eligible for listing in the National Register of Historic Places in 1978.[2] MWAA’s overview and business‑information pages likewise state that the Main Terminal is eligible for listing on the National Register of Historic Places.[13][14][75]

A PBS American Masters feature on Eero Saarinen describes the Dulles terminal as a “revolutionary” design that redefined airport typology by replacing long concourses with a single monumental hall and a mobile‑lounge system.[21][68] The feature interprets the sweeping roof and canted concrete supports as a formal expression of speed, movement, and technological optimism, and emphasizes how the roof appears to float above the glass walls.[21][22][68] It also highlights Saarinen’s integration of architecture, industrial design, and graphic systems—including furniture, ticket counters, signage, and vehicles—into a unified environment, presenting the terminal as a symbol of mid‑century modernism and jet‑age culture.[21]

Architectural reference works and retrospectives reinforce this assessment. An architecture‑history entry and a Modernist Collection essay describe the terminal as one of Saarinen’s late significant works, underscoring its sculptural roof, inclined concrete piers, extensive glass walls set back beneath the roof overhang, and open interior volume.[16][23][24] These sources frame the terminal as an exemplar of modernist “total design” and as an influential precedent in airport architecture.[16][23][24]

Brian Edwards’s study *The Modern Airport Terminal – New Approaches to Airport Architecture* likewise treats Dulles as an early classic of modern airport design and a turning point in purpose‑designed jet‑age terminals. Edwards notes the building’s single monumental hall, long‑span cable‑supported roof with inclined concrete supports, continuous glass enclosure, and the mobile‑lounge concept for flexible aircraft stands.[23] The book also comments that as air traffic grew, the mobile‑lounge system encountered efficiency challenges, illustrating how Dulles’s original operational concept had to adapt over time.[23]

Contemporary media continue to highlight the main terminal’s design reputation. A Simple Flying article describes the terminal as “widely revered,” outlining its distinctive roof form and the mobile‑lounge concept and presenting it as a defining feature of Washington Dulles International Airport.[76][77] A WTTW feature associated with the series “10 Buildings That Changed America” profiles Dulles as a significant American building completed in 1962, emphasizing the catenary‑curve roof, the openness of the interior hall, and the innovative use of mobile lounges.[15]

While these sources consistently portray the Dulles main terminal as architecturally important and culturally symbolic, the available evidence in this set does not include systematic data on public‑opinion surveys, detailed preservation‑policy debates, or extensive documentation of media appearances beyond the profiled features. The discussion here therefore focuses on documented institutional recognitions (such as the AIA Honor Award and National Register eligibility determinations) and representative appraisals from architectural and cultural commentators.[2][13][16][21][23][24][75]
